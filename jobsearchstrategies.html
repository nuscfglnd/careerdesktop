01 Networking

Networking is about making contact, building and maintaining new professional relationships. Networking has immense social and professional value for your career and can open doors to opportunities.

Finding a job via network referrals is far more efficient than going through the usual process of applying for an advertised job. 80% of the opportunities are not formally posted online (Collamer, 2013).

Why is Networking Important?

1

Networking allows you to multiply your career opportunities as your networks invite you to apply for roles not yet advertised 

2

By talking to people from different companies, you expand your knowledge on an industry or subject and learn about what fields interest you.

3

Applying your networking skills helps you to build professional confidence and interpersonal skills, which are desired capabilities that employers look out for during job interviews.

Did you know that you already have a network?

Your network of connections includes family members, friends, classmates and many more. Start with the connections listed in the diagram below. As you engage your network, recognise the value in your own set of knowledge such as fresh perspectives on current issues and work with the mindset that you are naturally building relationships through reciprocity and common interests. 

Here are some suggestions to get you started:

Professors and Lecturers

Setup an appointment with your lecturer to ask questions about a specific industry or job function you are interested in. Even if they are unable to assist you in your specific area of interest, they may be able to direct you someone who can.

Personal Contacts

Look back at your experiences and the connections you made throughout university and all other aspects of your life. Make a list of people you can invite for a coffee chat to gain some career advice and industry insights. They may go on to introduce you to other people that can contribute to your job search.

Alumni Events

Interacting with seniors is an excellent way to practice your networking skills and to gain insight into their experiences. You can ask them about their work culture, challenges they face, the reason they enjoy working in an organization and in so doing, gain clarity and insights on jobs that might appeal to you.

Career Events

The Centre for Future-ready Graduates offers a range of different career events, giving you the opportunity to engage with prospective employers and make direct connections with recruiters. All scheduled career events are published and announced via email and our weekly e-newsletters.

Career Advisors

Set up an in-person meeting on TalentConnect with your dedicated and professional Career Advisors to explore and plan your career journey. They provide support in a variety of ways, such as providing career consultation, résumé critique, mock interviews as well as internship and networking advisory. 

4 Tips for Networking

1

Research on the event and prepare your conversation topics 

Determine what you want to get out of the event, such as information on company culture or more about an industry or job role that interests you. 

Then, identify who you would like to speak to. Use LinkedIn to find out more about the company and the people you would like to approach so that you can identify topics of conversation.

Prepare some questions beforehand.

2

Open a conversation with your prepared elevator pitch and keep the conversation flowing 

A well-prepared elevator pitch (a short spiel about yourself) helps you introduce yourself confidently. Then, utilise the questions or discussion points you had prepared to keep the conversation smooth and flowing.

3

Use open-ended questions 

By asking close-ended questions like, ‘do you like your job’, you are inviting a very short response such as yes or no. Instead, ask an open-ended question like, ‘what do you like about your job’ that allows the person you are speaking to say much more.

4

Develop and maintain relationships, not contacts 

Be authentic and focus on the quality of these encounters rather than the quantity to make each networking moment meaningful. Exit the conversation gracefully and thank the listener for his or her time and views.

You can suggest continuing your conversation online or staying in touch through social media. Make a positive impression by following up after the event by recapping what you learn from the conversation and how you applied his or her advice. Follow up periodically with a friendly email or by sharing interesting articles and events that may be of interest.

02 Informational Interview

An informational interview allows you to gather specific information about job roles and industries that interest you, for instance: technical skills required for specific roles, job insights, hiring trends etc. The more you learn about an industry and job roles, the easier it will be to decide if that might be the right path for you.

Informational interviews serve to broaden your understanding of different career fields and expand your network of professional contacts.

3 Tips for Informational Interviews

Before

Begin by sending a brief email explaining how you found their contact details. Explain your interest in learning more about their field and request for a suitable date and time. Be sure to also briefly state how you became interested in the specified industry and be clear about your goals for the meeting. Be open to connecting via a phone conversation or email if the contact is unable to meet you.

Get help with writing an email template

Dear [Name],
I’m _Name__
from _NUS/Faculty/Year__.
I noticed _Observation about the work the person does__ and
I’m interested in _area of interest/purpose in connecting__.
Do you have time for a chat, either in person or over the phone so that I can ask you some questions and get some career advice?
I look forward to hearing from you soon. Thank you. 

During

During the interview, be prepared and clear about the questions you plan to ask. The average time for an informational interview is about 20 to 30 minutes. You may bring along your resume but only provide it if asked. Be prepared to answer questions about your technical and transferable skills, internship experiences, club activities and other interests.

After

After the interview, send a thank you note within 24 hours to show your appreciation, highlighting what you have learnt from the conversation. It is also a good idea to ask for referrals to other contacts you might be able to share industry insights with you.

03 Speculative Applications

A speculative application refers to writing in directly to employers to express your interest in a particular role. Finding a named contact is the number one rule of making a speculative job application. ‘Dear Sir/Madam’ emails sent to HR departments are likely to be ignored You may be able to get a specific referral from networking, but if not, a quick phone call to the company to ask for the name of the recruitment personnel will enable you to personalize your email. Alternatively a LinkedIn search is likely to list people in the company you are intending to write to.

Here are some pointers to increase the success of a speculative application

  • Start with some brief and engaging information about your skills, passions and interest. Explain your motivation for applying to the company or department.
  • Tailor your resume and cover letter to persuade the company that you are a strategic hire
  • Research into their recent projects and activities so you can outline your understanding and highlight how you can contribute in your cover letter.
  • Follow up with a phone call or an email after a few days to improve your chances of success

05 Job Portals

Link
